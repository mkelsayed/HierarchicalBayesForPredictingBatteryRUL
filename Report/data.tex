\section{Data Overview} \label{sec:dataoverview}
The primary data for this study is sourced from the battery cycling datasets hosted on \texttt{data.matr.io}~\cite{Matrdata}.. 
The consolidated dataset consists of 169 Lithium-ion phosphate (LFP) cells cycled under varying fast-charging conditions. These cells represent 78 distinct charging protocols and have lifetimes that vary accordingly.

\subsection{Data Structure and Extraction}
The raw data, originally stored in MATLAB format, was ingested into a Python-based workflow following the methodology from Attia et al.'s GitHub~\cite{Braatz2019github}. 
The dataset is organized hierarchically: at the cell level, each battery is indexed by a unique key and carries a charging protocol string and a cycle life — the number of cycles until end-of-life (EoL) is reached. 
Below this, cycle-by-cycle summary measurements (capacity, internal resistance, temperature) and intra-cycle time-series (voltage, current, capacity curves) are accessible for each cell. 
A full description of available variables is provided in the Appendix.

\subsection{Data Partitioning and Grouping Logic}\label{sec:groupinglogic}
Zhou and Howey \cite{ZHOU20236117} partitioned the data into eight groups based on their average C-rate and calculated the variance partition coefficient (VPC). Specifically, they calculated:
\begin{align*}
\text{VPC} &= \frac{\sigma^2_{\text{group}}}{\sigma^2_{\text{individual}} + \sigma^2_{\text{group}}} \\
\sigma^2_{\text{group}} &= \text{var}\left(\bar{u}_j - \bar{u}\right), \quad \bar{u} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{k} \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} y_{ji} \\
\sigma^2_{\text{individual}} &= \text{var}\left(y_{ji} - \bar{u}_j\right), \quad \bar{u}_j = \frac{1}{n_j} \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} y_{ji}
\end{align*}
where $\sigma^2_{\text{group}}$ represents lifetime variance between groups and $\sigma^2_{\text{individual}}$ represents the total within-group lifetime variance. 
They obtained a VPC of 74.5\%, implying that between-group variance accounts for the majority of total lifetime variance. It follows that any statistical model which ignores group-level
variation would be discarding useful information about the data's variability, while fitting separate statistical models on each group would ignore the remaining within group variability.
A hierarchical Bayesian model addresses both by partially pooling estimates across groups.

\subsection{Grouping Results} \label{subsec:GroupingResults}
Following Zhou and Howey ~\cite{ZHOU20236117}, we first applied constrained K-means clustering on average charging C-rate. 
While this produced groups with low within-group C-rate variance (see Figure 1), the resulting VPC was only 38.973\%. 
We subsequently grouped cells by their unique charging policy string and optimized the grouping via a hill-climbing algorithm — iteratively reassigning policy strings across the eight groups to maximise VPC — achieving 74.406\%.
The hill-climb grouping exhibits higher within-group C-rate variance but lower within-group EoL variance (see Figure 2).
