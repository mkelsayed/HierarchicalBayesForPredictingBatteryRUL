\section{Experiment}

The analysis was completed in Python, and the hierarchical Bayesian model was built in Python using the PyMC package \cite{pymc2023}. 

First, the data set was processed following section \ref{sec:dataoverview}. Early cycle features described in table \ref{table:features} were extracted and the data was partitioned into 8 groups as described in section \ref{subsec:GroupingResults}. 
The following experiment uses the hill-climbing grouping, which yielded a higher VPC ($74.5\%$ vs.\ $39.0\%$ for K-means) — indicating greater between-group variance 
relative to total variance (section~\ref{sec:groupinglogic}), and thus a more statistically justified hierarchical structure. the HBM under K-means grouping confirmed this, showing no improvement over the baseline.
The data was split into training and test sets of 135 and 34 cells respectively, stratified by cycling group $g$. A 5-fold cross-validation was performed 4 times (yielding 20 error estimates) to assess the generalizability of the model.

\section{Results and Discussion}

The performance of the Hierarchical Bayesian model is compared against two baselines: a ridge regression model with just the features in Table \ref{table:features}, and one including the cycling group $g$ as well. Root mean error squared (RMSE) and mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) are used as metrics to evaluate the performance of the models. We consider MAPE as an additional metric because RMSE is significantly influenced by outliers.
\begin{align*}
    \text{RMSE} &= \sqrt{\frac{\sum^N_{i=1} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{N}} \\
    \text{MAPE} &= \frac{1}{N}\sum^N_{i=1} \left| \frac{y_i - \hat{y}_i}{y_i} \right|
\end{align*}

To assess model validity, table \ref{tab:mcmcresults} shows MCMC convergence diagnostics for the final model fit. All parameters achieved $\hat{R} \leq 1.01$ and bulk effective sample sizes above 894, indicating that the chains mixed well and the posterior estimates are reliable \cite{vehtari2021rank}. The observation noise parameter $\sigma_y$ was tightly estimated at $0.55 \pm 0.04$ (standardized units) with an ESS of 3433, showing high posterior certainty about the residual variance.

The hyperprior slope $\gamma_{\text{slope},0}$, governing how mean C-rate modulates the group-level intercept, has a posterior mean of $-3.27$ with a 94\% HDI of $[-4.91, -1.55]$, which excludes zero. This provides credible evidence that groups with higher average charge rates are associated with shorter battery lifetimes at the group level, and justifies the hierarchical structure of the model. Furthermore, $\sigma_{\theta,0} = 0.47$ with HDI $[0.13, 0.94]$ confirms that meaningful variation in baseline lifetime persists across groups beyond what C-rate alone explains. In contrast, the hyperprior slopes for the early-cycle features $\gamma_{\text{slope},1{-}3}$ all have HDIs that include zero, indicating that the regression coefficients for F1, F2, and F3 are shared across groups rather than modulated by cycling condition. The hierarchy therefore operates primarily through group intercepts, while feature effects generalize across charging protocols.
