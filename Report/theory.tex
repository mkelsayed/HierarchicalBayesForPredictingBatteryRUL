\section{Introduction to Hierarchical Bayes}

\subsection{Multilevel Modelling}
To motivate hierarchical modelling, consider first the standard linear regression setting:
\[
y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \epsilon
\]

Now suppose the data naturally contains J groups — for example, homes prices across J states, where the relationship between price and square footage may differ by state. 
This suggests allowing group-specific parameters:
\[
y_i = \alpha_{j[i]} + \beta_{j[i]} x_i + \epsilon_i
\]
where $j$ is the group index. In the Bayesian framework, unknown parameters are treated as random variables and assigned prior distributions reflecting beliefs about their likely values.
Accordingly, $(\alpha_{j[i]}, \beta_{j[i]})$ are each assigned a prior — for example, they may be jointly normally distributed \cite{GelmanHill2007}:
\[
\begin{pmatrix} 
\alpha_j \\ 
\beta_j 
\end{pmatrix} 
\sim N \left( 
\begin{pmatrix} 
\mu_{\alpha} \\ 
\mu_{\beta} 
\end{pmatrix}, \mathbf{\Sigma} 
\right), \text{ for } j = 1, \dots, J
\]

\subsection{Pooling Tradeoff}
In the example above, the multilevel regression may use the same parameters for all groups (complete pooling), or fit entirely separate models for each group with no information shared across them (no pooling). 
In the latter case, the parameter distribution is specified independently for each group:
\[
\begin{pmatrix} 
\alpha_j \\ 
\beta_j 
\end{pmatrix} 
\sim N \left( 
\begin{pmatrix} 
\mu_{\alpha, j} \\ 
\mu_{\beta, j} 
\end{pmatrix}, \mathbf{\Sigma}_j 
\right), \quad j = 1, \dots, J
\]

\subsection{Pooling Tradeoff: County Radon Levels}
\begin{figure}[h]

    \caption{Estimates $\pm$ standard errors for average log radon levels in Minnesota counties versus number of observations per county: (a) no-pooling, (b) partial pooling. Counties with fewer observations have more variable estimates and larger standard errors under no pooling, illustrating how small samples produce unreliable county-level estimates.}
\end{figure}

Gelman et al.~\cite{GelmanHill2007} analyzed a dataset consisting of radon levels for the 85 counties of Minnesota. The objective was to estimate the distribution of the radon levels of the houses within each county. The first proposed approach was to calculate statistics (e.g., average) using all homes from the sample (complete pooling). This approach does not consider variability among counties and complicates the task of estimating county-specific distributions. 

The second proposed approach was to calculate the average log-radon levels in each county (no pooling). With this approach, the authors found that the highest county-specific average log radon level of 85 was much larger than the values from different counties. 
However, this outlier may not be representative of the actual average log radon levels of its county, since it was obtained from a sample of only two observations. No pooling therefore produces unreliable estimates for data-sparse groups, motivating a middle ground.

\subsection{Partial Pooling}
Gelman et al.~\cite{GelmanHill2007} provided a middle ground between complete and no pooling, which they called partial pooling. Specifically, they defined the observation-level model as:
\[
y_i \sim N(\alpha_{j[i]} + \beta x_i, \sigma^2_y), \quad i = 1, \dots, n
\]
with the group-level model:
\[
\alpha_j \sim N(\mu_\alpha, \sigma^2_\alpha), \quad j = 1, \dots, J
\]
Together they form a two-level structure: in the first equation, each observation is normally distributed around its group's intercept plus a shared slope; in the second, the group intercepts are drawn from a common population distribution. 

The two-level structure creates dependency between group-level intercepts through the shared prior $N(\mu_\alpha, \sigma^2_\alpha)$. Specifically, each $\alpha_j$ is estimated from its group's data and also influenced by all other groups. This pulls data-sparse groups toward the population mean $\mu_\alpha$ while leaving data-rich groups largely unaffected. The strength of the pull is also determined by $\sigma_\alpha$, which is learned from the data.

\subsection{Hierarchical Bayes}
In the multilevel modelling example above, the parameters $\mu_\alpha$ and $\sigma^2_\alpha$ are unknown. In Bayesian statistics, these parameters are treated as random variables which are assigned prior distributions. 
Placing priors on these parameters gives the full hierarchical Bayesian model:

\begin{align*}
&\textbf{Level 3 (Hyperpriors):} & \mu_\alpha &\sim p(\mu_\alpha), \quad \sigma^2_\alpha \sim p(\sigma^2_\alpha) \\[6pt]
&\textbf{Level 2 (Group prior):} & \alpha_j &\sim N(\mu_\alpha, \sigma^2_\alpha), \quad j = 1, \dots, J \\[6pt]
&\textbf{Level 1 (Observation model):} & y_i &\sim N(\alpha_{j[i]} + \beta x_i, \sigma^2_y), \quad i = 1, \dots, n
\end{align*}

\section{Hierarchical Bayesian Model for Battery Lifetime Prediction}
\subsection{Model Details}
Following Zhou \& Howey~\cite{ZHOU20236117}, we instantiate the three-level HBM for battery lifetime prediction. The response variable $y_{ji}$ denotes the EoL (in days) for cell $i$ in group $j$. 
At the individual level, each cell is represented by a feature vector $\mathbf{x}_{ji} \in \mathbb{R}^4$ constructed from early-cycle measurements; at the group level, each cycling condition 
group is represented by $\mathbf{g}_j \in \mathbb{R}^2$, which encodes the group's average C-rate. A full description of model variables and features is provided in the Appendix.

\begin{align*}
&\textbf{Level 3 (Hyperpriors):} & \boldsymbol{\gamma} &\sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, 10\mathbf{I}) \\[6pt]
&\textbf{Level 2 (Cycling conditions):} & \boldsymbol{\theta}_j &\sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\gamma}^\top \mathbf{g}_j, \sigma^2_j), \quad j = 1, \dots, J \\[6pt]
&\textbf{Level 1 (Individual cells):} & y_{ji} &\sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_j^\top \mathbf{x}_{ji}, \sigma^2_y), \quad i = 1, \dots, n_j
\end{align*}

The three-level structure encodes two sources of variability simultaneously: individual cell variability within each group (Level 1) and variability in 
how features relate to EoL across cycling conditions (Level 2). Crucially, at Level 2, the group coefficients $\boldsymbol{\theta}_j$ are not estimated 
independently — they are drawn from a common distribution centred at $\boldsymbol{\gamma}^\top \mathbf{g}_j$, allowing for information sharing across groups.

\subsection{Inference}
Inference follows a sequential process. First, the posterior over the hyperparameters is estimated via MCMC~\cite{ZHOU20236117}:
\[
P(\boldsymbol{\gamma} \mid \{Y\}) \propto P(\boldsymbol{\gamma}) \prod_{j=1}^{J} P(Y_j \mid \boldsymbol{\gamma})
\]
where $P(\boldsymbol{\gamma}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, 10\mathbf{I})$ is the hyperprior. Since the group parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}_j$ are unknown at this stage, they are treated as random variables and marginalised out of the likelihood:
\[
P(Y_j \mid \boldsymbol{\gamma}) = \int P(Y_j \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_j) \cdot P(\boldsymbol{\theta}_j \mid \boldsymbol{\gamma}) \, d\boldsymbol{\theta}_j
\]
The resulting posterior $P(\boldsymbol{\gamma} \mid \{Y\})$ has no closed form due to the intractable normalising constant $P(\{Y\})$; MCMC is therefore used to obtain samples from it. 
These samples are then used as a prior to obtain the group-level posteriors $P(\boldsymbol{\theta}_j \mid \boldsymbol{\gamma}, Y_j)$ via Bayes' rule~\cite{ZHOU20236117}. Starting from the joint distribution, we have:
$$P(\boldsymbol{\theta}_j, \gamma, Y_j) = P(Y_j \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_j) \cdot P(\boldsymbol{\theta}_j \mid \gamma) \cdot P(\gamma)$$
Conditioning on $\gamma$ and $Y_j$ and normalizing gives:

\begin{align*}
P(\boldsymbol{\theta}_j \mid \gamma, Y_j) &= \frac{P(Y_j,\, \boldsymbol{\theta}_j,\, \gamma)}{P(Y_j \mid \gamma)} \\[10pt]
                                           &= \frac{P(Y_j \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_j,\, \gamma) \cdot P(\boldsymbol{\theta}_j,\, \gamma)}{P(Y_j \mid \gamma)} \\[10pt]
                                           &= \frac{P(Y_j \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_j) \cdot P(\boldsymbol{\theta}_j \mid \gamma) \cdot P(\gamma)}{P(Y_j \mid \gamma)}
\end{align*}

where in the third line we use the fact that once $\boldsymbol{\theta}_j$ is known, $\gamma$ carries no additional information about $Y_j$, so $P(Y_j \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_j,\, \gamma) = P(Y_j \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_j)$.
